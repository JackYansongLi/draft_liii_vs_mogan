\paragraph{Related work}

{\color{red}\textbf{LLM-driven \LaTeX{} generation and its challenges.}}
As LLMs are increasingly applied to scientific writing, the gap between their capabilities and \LaTeX{}'s structural complexity has become a central concern. Kale and Nadadur~\cite{kale2025texpert} introduced the \textbf{TeXpert} benchmark, revealing that even state-of-the-art LLMs achieve only 15\% accuracy on complex \LaTeX{} generation tasks, with logical errors accounting for 54\% of failures. Ling et al.~\cite{ling_table2latex_2025} proposed a reinforcement-learning approach for table-to-\LaTeX{} conversion but reported persistent inefficiency due to \LaTeX{}'s high token cost. Xia et al.~\cite{xia2024docgenome} conducted large-scale benchmarks showing that Equation-to-\LaTeX{} and Table-to-\LaTeX{} tasks remain challenging (Edit Distance $> 0.21$), attributing this to the high information entropy of \LaTeX{} source code. {\color{red}Lu et al.~\cite{lu2024aiscientist} demonstrated a fully automated scientific discovery pipeline (\textbf{The AI Scientist}), in which \LaTeX{} document generation emerged as a critical bottleneck: the model cannot perceive the rendered PDF layout, frequently producing overflowing tables, misplaced figures, and placeholder text---failures rooted in the opacity of \LaTeX{}'s batch compilation model.}

{\color{red}\textbf{Token efficiency and document representations for LLMs.}}
{\color{red}The cost of processing \LaTeX{} in LLM workflows extends beyond generation accuracy. Paster et al.~\cite{paster2023openwebmath} constructed a 14.7B-token mathematical dataset from web sources, highlighting the complexity of extracting and normalizing \LaTeX{} content from heterogeneous formats. Lin et al.~\cite{lin2024accurate} showed that applying LLMs directly to document processing incurs prohibitive computational costs, achieving up to $30\times$ cost reduction through semantic hierarchical indexing---underscoring the need for more efficient document representations. Filho~\cite{filho_doa_2026} proposed documentation-oriented architectures using Markdown as a coordination layer for multi-agent systems, arguing that verbose markup languages impose unnecessary overhead on AI-driven workflows. These findings collectively point to a fundamental tension: \LaTeX{}'s syntactic redundancy (e.g., \texttt{\textbackslash frac\{a\}\{b\}} vs.\ \texttt{\{a \textbackslash over b\}}) inflates token consumption while reducing predictability for next-token prediction, a problem we quantify via fine-tuning experiments in Section~\ref{sec:eff-in-sft}.}

{\color{red}\textbf{Alternative typesetting paradigms.}}
{\color{red}Dissatisfaction with \TeX{}'s batch model has motivated two distinct lines of alternative systems. On the markup side, Typst~\cite{madje2023typst} replaces \TeX{}'s macro expansion with pure functions and an incremental compiler written in Rust, achieving sub-second preview updates for large documents~\cite{madje2023typst_thesis}. While Typst addresses compilation speed and error localization, it remains a source-editing paradigm without WYSIWYG capabilities. On the structured editing side, Van der Hoeven~\cite{van_der_hoeven_gnu_2001} demonstrated with GNU \TeX{}macs that WYSIWYG and structured mathematical typesetting are not inherently incompatible---a position long contested in the \TeX{} community. Mittelbach et al.~\cite{mittelbach2004latex} documented the engineering complexity accumulated over decades of \LaTeX{} package development, providing context for why incremental reform (such as the \LaTeX{}3/\texttt{expl3} effort discussed in Section~\ref{sec:ux}) has struggled to resolve the system's architectural limitations. Xia et al.~\cite{xia2024overleafcopilot} integrated LLMs into the Overleaf platform, but found that the underlying \LaTeX{} compilation model imposes hard constraints on what AI-assisted editing can achieve---motivating exploration of fundamentally different document architectures such as the tree-based \texttt{.tmu} format introduced in Section~\ref{sec:mogan}.}
